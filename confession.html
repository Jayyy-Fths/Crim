Key Interrogation and Confession Concepts

Custodial Interrogation

Custodial interrogation is questioning by police after a person has been taken into custody or deprived of freedom in a significant way – a tense dialogue that tests the limits of human resilience and rights.

Custody exists when a reasonable person would not feel free to leave. Miranda warnings are required before questioning a person in custody. The test is objective: would a reasonable person feel free to terminate the interrogation and leave?

Miranda Rights

Miranda rights inform suspects of their right to remain silent, right to an attorney, and that anything said can be used against them. Required before custodial interrogation.

The famous warning: "You have the right to remain silent. Anything you say can and will be used against you in court. You have the right to an attorney. If you cannot afford an attorney, one will be provided for you."

Voluntary vs. Coerced Confessions

Voluntary confessions are given freely without coercion. Coerced confessions are obtained through threats, promises, or physical/psychological pressure and are inadmissible.

Courts examine the totality of circumstances: the suspect's characteristics, the conduct of police, and the environment of the interrogation. Coerced confessions violate the Fifth Amendment's protection against self-incrimination.

Right to Counsel

The Sixth Amendment provides the right to counsel during criminal cases. Once invoked, police must stop questioning until counsel is present.

The right attaches once formal charges are filed. If a suspect asks for an attorney, all interrogation must cease. Police may not re-initiate questioning until counsel is provided or the suspect voluntarily waives the right.

False Confessions

False confessions occur when innocent people admit to crimes they didn't commit, often due to coercion, mental illness, or interrogation tactics. This is a major cause of wrongful convictions.

Studies indicate that about 25% of wrongful convictions involve false confessions. Factors contributing include: prolonged interrogation, sleep deprivation, isolation, manipulation, and real or implied promises of leniency.

The Science of Interrogation

Interrogations often employ psychological tactics like the Reid Technique, which builds rapport, presents evidence, and minimizes the crime's severity to obtain a confession.

Research shows that prolonged questioning can lead to false confessions, especially among vulnerable groups like juveniles, people with mental illness, or those with intellectual disabilities. Understanding these dynamics is crucial for preventing miscarriages of justice.

False Confession Factors

Factors that Contribute to False Confessions:

  • Prolonged interrogation (several hours)
  • Mental illness or cognitive impairment
  • Youth (especially juveniles)
  • Intoxication
  • Deception about evidence
  • Interrogation in isolation
  • Fear of physical harm

Miranda v. Arizona (1966)

Background: Ernesto Miranda was arrested in his house, brought to the police station, and questioned by police in connection with a kidnapping and rape. After two hours of interrogation, police obtained a written confession from Miranda.

Significance: This landmark case requires police to inform suspects of their rights before custodial interrogation. The famous "Miranda warning" ensures suspects know they can remain silent and have an attorney present. It's one of the most recognized legal protections in American law.

Key Principle: Prosecution may not use statements stemming from custodial interrogation of the defendant unless it demonstrates that procedural safeguards were used to secure the privilege against self-incrimination. These safeguards include warnings about the right to remain silent and the right to counsel.

Escobedo v. Illinois (1964)

Background: Danny Escobedo was arrested and questioned about his brother's murder. He requested an attorney multiple times, but police denied his requests and continued questioning. Eventually, his brother's statement was used to convict Escobedo.

Significance: This case was a precursor to Miranda and established that criminal suspects have a right to counsel during police interrogations. It recognized that denial of the right to counsel during interrogation violates the Sixth Amendment.

Key Principle: When a police investigation shifts focus to a suspect and the suspect requests an attorney, police must stop interrogation or any further attempts to obtain a confession. Failure to honor this request makes any subsequent confession inadmissible.

Rhode Island v. Innis (1980)

Background: Thomas Innis was arrested for robbery. After invoking his right to remain silent, officers began discussing among themselves where to find the missing shotgun. Innis interrupted and revealed the gun's location.

Significance: This case defined what constitutes "interrogation" under Miranda, clarifying that it includes not just express questioning, but also any words or actions the police should know are likely to elicit an incriminating response.

Key Principle: "Interrogation" under Miranda refers not only to express questioning, but also to any police practices that are designed, conducted, or may reasonably be expected to evoke an incriminating response from the suspect. This includes strategic conversation with suspects.

Michigan v. Mosley (1975)

Background: Detectives questioned Mosley about robberies. After he invoked his right to remain silent, they ceased questioning. Two hours later, another detective questioned him about unrelated crimes after giving him fresh Miranda warnings.

Significance: This case established rules for when police can re-initiate questioning after a suspect has invoked the right to remain silent. It balances the suspect's rights with legitimate law enforcement needs.

Key Principle: A suspect who invokes the right to remain silent may be re-interrogated if: (1) the suspect's right to cut off questioning was scrupulously honored, and (2) the re-interrogation is preceded by fresh Miranda warnings about the right to remain silent and to have an attorney present.

Colorado v. Connelly (1986)

Background: Francis Connelly approached a police officer and confessed to a murder. He was experiencing severe mental illness. The trial court suppressed his confession as involuntary because of his mental state, but the Supreme Court reversed.

Significance: This case clarified that involuntary confessions are those coerced by police action, not those influenced by a suspect's internal psychological state. Mental illness alone doesn't make a confession involuntary.

Key Principle: The Miranda exclusionary rule and the Fifth Amendment's self-incrimination clause are only concerned with governmental coercion. Involuntariness requires police overreach that overcomes the suspect's free will. Voluntary confessions to private citizens don't trigger Miranda.

Yarborough v. Alvarado (2004)

Background: Police brought 18-year-old Michael Alvarado to the station for questioning about a robbery. He was questioned for two hours in a small room with two detectives. He eventually confessed. The question was whether he was in "custody" requiring Miranda warnings.

Significance: This case established factors for determining whether a suspect is in custody for Miranda purposes. It provides guidance on how courts should analyze whether a reasonable person would feel free to leave.

Key Principle: Courts must consider: (1) the language used to summon the individual, (2) the physical surroundings of the interrogation, (3) the extent to which the individual is confronted with evidence of guilt, and (4) whether the individual is free to leave. Age and experience may also be relevant.
