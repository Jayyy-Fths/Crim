Key Arrest Concepts

Definition of Arrest

An arrest is the act of apprehending and taking a person into custody by a law enforcement officer in response to a criminal offense. It involves the deprivation of liberty and is a formal accusation of a crime – a moment that echoes with profound consequences for all involved.

Arrest differs from a mere stop because it involves a significant deprivation of freedom. The key test is whether a reasonable person would believe they are not free to leave.

Probable Cause

Probable cause is the reasonable belief that a crime has been committed and that the person to be arrested committed it. It requires more than mere suspicion but less than evidence beyond a reasonable doubt.

Probable cause is determined by examining the totality of circumstances, including observed behavior, witness statements, and officer expertise. It's the constitutional minimum for arrest.

Arrest Warrants

An arrest warrant is a written order from a judge authorizing law enforcement to arrest a specific person for a specific crime. It must be based on probable cause and supported by an affidavit.

The warrant must contain the name of the accused, the crime charged, and be signed by a neutral magistrate. However, many arrests occur without warrants under specific exceptions.

Warrantless Arrests

Warrantless arrests are allowed in certain situations, such as when a crime is committed in the officer's presence, or when there is probable cause and exigent circumstances prevent obtaining a warrant.

Exigent circumstances include: hot pursuit of a suspect, risk of evidence destruction, danger to officer or public safety, or the suspect is likely to escape.

Use of Force Standards

The use of force by police must be reasonable and proportional to the threat posed. Standards are guided by the Fourth Amendment and cases like Graham v. Connor, emphasizing objective reasonableness.

Officers may use force when necessary to overcome resistance, effect an arrest, or protect themselves and others. The force continuum ranges from verbal commands to deadly force.

Arrest Statistics and Trends

According to recent FBI data, over 10 million arrests occur annually in the United States. Arrests for drug offenses, property crimes, and violent crimes dominate these numbers.

Disparities exist: African Americans are arrested at 2-3 times the rate of whites for similar offenses. These statistics raise ongoing questions about bias and systemic inequalities in the criminal justice system.

Terry v. Ohio (1968)

Background: Detective Martin McFadden observed two men repeatedly walking past a store window and conferring with each other. Suspecting they were planning a robbery, he approached and frisked them, finding weapons.

Significance: This landmark case established the "stop and frisk" doctrine, creating an exception to the Fourth Amendment's warrant requirement. It recognized that police need less than probable cause to conduct a brief investigatory stop.

Key Principle: A police officer may stop a person if they have reasonable suspicion that the person has committed or is about to commit a crime, and may pat down for weapons if they have reasonable suspicion that the person is armed and dangerous.

Payton v. New York (1980)

Background: New York police officers entered Payton's apartment without a warrant and arrested him for murder. They found a gun in plain view during the entry.

Significance: This case strengthened Fourth Amendment protections for the home, establishing that police generally need an arrest warrant before entering a suspect's home, even when they have probable cause to arrest.

Key Principle: The home enjoys the highest degree of protection against governmental intrusion. Warrantless entries into homes to make arrests are presumptively unreasonable unless exigent circumstances exist.

Graham v. Connor (1989)

Background: Dethorne Graham, a diabetic, ran into a store to get orange juice when he realized he was having an insulin reaction. Officers attempted to restrain him, and he filed a excessive force lawsuit.

Significance: This case established the nationwide standard for evaluating Fourth Amendment claims of excessive force by police. It removed confusion among lower courts about which constitutional amendment applied.

Key Principle: All claims of excessive force are analyzed under the Fourth Amendment's "objective reasonableness" standard. Courts must consider: (1) severity of the crime, (2) whether suspect poses immediate threat, (3) whether suspect is actively resisting, and (4) the availability of alternatives.

Tennessee v. Garner (1985)

Background: Memphis police officerLt. Elton Hyder III shot and killed a 15-year-old burglary suspect, Garner, as he fled over a fence. The shooting was department policy allowing deadly force against fleeing felons.

Significance: This case ended the common law "fleeing felon" rule that allowed officers to shoot any fleeing felon. It established constitutional limits on the use of deadly force during arrests.

Key Principle: Deadly force may not be used to prevent escape unless the officer has probable cause to believe the suspect poses a significant threat of death or serious physical injury to the officer or others. The nature of the crime (violent vs. non-violent) matters.
