The Fourth Amendment

The Fourth Amendment protects against unreasonable searches and seizures. It requires warrants based on probable cause and describes what constitutes a search – a cornerstone of personal dignity and autonomy.

Search Warrants

A search warrant is a court order authorizing law enforcement to search a specific location for specific items. It must be supported by probable cause and describe the place and items to be searched.

Exclusionary Rule

The exclusionary rule prohibits the use of illegally obtained evidence in court. It deters police misconduct and protects constitutional rights.

Plain View Doctrine

The plain view doctrine allows seizure of evidence that is in plain view during a lawful search or observation, without a separate warrant.

Stop and Frisk

Stop and frisk allows police to briefly detain and pat down suspects based on reasonable suspicion of a crime, as established in Terry v. Ohio.

Exceptions to the Warrant Requirement

Exceptions include consent, exigent circumstances, plain view, automobile exception, and searches incident to arrest.

Controversial Issues in Search and Seizure

Search and seizure laws often spark debate. The use of technology, like cell phone tracking or body cameras, raises privacy concerns. Cases involving racial profiling in traffic stops highlight ongoing tensions. Additionally, the "good faith" exception allows evidence from faulty warrants to be admitted, balancing justice with practicality.

Emerging issues include digital searches: Does law enforcement need a warrant to access cloud data? These questions challenge us to adapt constitutional protections to modern technology.

Search and Seizure Images

Mapp v. Ohio (1961)

This case extended the exclusionary rule to state courts. Evidence obtained through illegal searches cannot be used in criminal trials, ensuring that police follow constitutional procedures.

Katz v. United States (1967)

The Supreme Court redefined privacy under the Fourth Amendment. A search occurs when government action violates a person's reasonable expectation of privacy, not just physical trespass.
